State of Play: The Complex Landscape of Working with Children Checks Across Australia

Anyone who works with, cares for, or volunteers with children under the age of 18 is required to undertake screening known as a Working with Children Check (WWCC). By screening individuals involved with children, the Working with Children Check is one element of ensuring that children are protected from sexual or physical harm.

Throughout Australia, millions of employees and volunteers hold a Working with Children Check. Each state and territory has different legislation governing WWCCs. The result is a mish-mash of rules, regulations, exemptions and exclusions that can be a headache for both individuals and organisations trying to do the right thing and ensure compliance.

The goal of this piece is to provide a resource for organisations that engage staff and volunteers operating across multiple states and territories understand and meet their obligations.

Below you'll find:

  • The different requirements for WWCCs in each state and territory, things to watch out for and links to more information to help ensure you, your staff and volunteers are compliant;
  • The future of Working with Children Checks in Australia.

Victoria

What's it called?

Working with Children Check

What does it screen for?

Criminal history across an individual's lifetime - specifically sexual, violent or drug offences. More information.

Who needs one?

Any individual involved in ‘child related work'. This includes volunteers as well as employees. More information.

Where to get it?

You can apply for a check through the Victorian Department of Justice website here.

How long is it valid?

5 years.

Can I do child-related work in Victoria as a visitor?

Interstate visitors can do child-related work in Victoria without a Victorian Check for a period of up to 30 days in the same calendar year for:

  • Several events or occasions with an equivalent Check from their state or territory; or
  • Only one event or occasion without an equivalent Check from their state or territory.

More information.

Penalties

Organisations engaging individuals without a valid check can face fines of over $190,000. More information.

Things to watch out for...

Employees and volunteers require different checks in Victoria. Employees require the payment of a fee whilst volunteer checks are free. More information.

Registered teachers and police officers are exempt from requiring a WWCC in Victoria.

On 1 August 2017, the scope of those requiring WWCCs was broadened to include people who have oral, written or electronic communication with children as well as several other changes. More information.

New South Wales

What's it called?

Working with Children Check

What does it screen for?

It screens an individual's national criminal history check and a review of findings of workplace misconduct. More information.

Who needs one?

Any individual who is an employee or volunteer in ‘child-related work'. More information.

Where to get it?

You can apply for a check through the Office of the Children's Guardian here.

How long is it valid?

5 years.

Can I do child-related work in NSW as a visitor?

Interstate visitors:

  • Can work or volunteer at a one-off event such as a jamboree, sporting or religious event or tour, for up to 30 days a year without a NSW Working with Children Check;
  • Can work or volunteer in any child-related work for up to 30 days a year, if the person holds an interstate Working with Children Check, or is exempt from the requirement to have such a check in his or her home jurisdiction.

More Information.

Things to watch out for...

In NSW, an individual won't be issued with a physical card or certificate – instead the applicant will receive a WWCC number which they are required to share with organisations they're working with.

Queensland

What's it called?

Blue Card or Exemption Card.

What does it screen for?

An individual's past police and disciplinary records. More information.

Who needs one?

Paid employees and volunteers who work with children and young people must hold a blue card or an exemption card. More information.

Where to get it?

You can apply for a check through the Blue Card Services here.

How long is it valid?

3 years.

Can I do child-related work in Queensland as a visitor?

No. Queensland does not recognise working with children checks conducted outside of Queensland. More information.

Penalties

Organisations who engage with individuals without a valid check can face fines over $25,000 or up to 2 years' imprisonment. More information.

Individuals without a valid check can faces fines of over $60,000 or face up to 5 years' imprisonment. More information.

Things to watch out for...

In Queensland, registered teachers or police officers do not need to apply for a blue card and instead should apply for an exemption card. More information.

South Australia

What's it called?

Child-Related Employment Screening.

What does it screen for?

Child-Related Employment Screening takes into account the applicant's national criminal history record as well as a wider range of information. More information.

Who needs one?

An employee or volunteer who has regular contact with children or working in close proximity to children on a regular basis. More information.

Where to get it?

You can apply for a check through the DSCI Screening Unit here.

How long is it valid?

3 years.

Can I do child-related work in SA as a visitor?

No. A Check from interstate is not recognised in South Australia as being the same to a clearance by the DCSI Screening Unit. More information.

Things to watch out for...

Both employees and volunteers must pay to get a screening check, however volunteers and students on placements pays a discounted fee. More information.

The South Australian Parliament recently passed laws to bring South Australia's screening more in line with other states. More information. The new laws are yet to come into effect but will be similar in operation to Working with Children Checks in other states.

Western Australia

What's it called?

Working with Children Check.

What does it screen for?

The criminal records and other relevant information about an individual who engages in child related work. More information.

Who needs one?

A WWCC is required by a person who engages in paid or unpaid work with children. More information.

Where to get it?

You cannot apply online for a WWCC in WA. WWCC application forms can be found at Australia Post outlets. More information.

How long is it valid?

3 years.

Can I do child-related work in WA as a visitor?

Interstate visitors can do child-related work in WA without a WWCC for a period of up to 14 days in any 12-month period if they hold a valid interstate check. More information.

Penalties

Organisations engaging individuals without a valid check can face fines of up to $60,000 and possible imprisonment. More information.

Individuals without a valid check can faces fines of up to $60,000 or face up to 5 years' imprisonment. More information.

Things to watch out for...

In Western Australia, it is the responsibility of the employer, volunteer organisation or education provider to sign off on a person's application form when they are about to start child-related work for the first time.

Northern Territory

What's it called?

Ochre Card.

What does it screen for?

The Ochre Card screens an individual's police history, employment records and reports about their recent behavior. More information.

Who needs one?

Any employee or volunteer that works with children in NT. More information.

Where to get it?

You can apply for a check here.

How long is it valid?

2 years.

Can I do child-related work in NT as a visitor?

You do not need any clearance if you are visiting the NT and volunteering with children for less than 14 days in a calendar year. More information.

Penalties

Organisations allowing someone to work or volunteer with children without a clearance can face fines of up to $76,500. Individuals can be fined up to the same amount. More information.

Things to watch out for...

Your employer can apply for limited exemption for you to start work while your application is being processed. More information.

Tasmania

What's it called?

Working with Vulnerable People Registration.

What does it screen for?

A person's national police records are assessed to see if they have charges or convictions that indicates if they pose a risk of harm to a child. More information.

Who needs one?

If you work or volunteer with children you need to apply for this registration, with some exceptions. More information.

Where to get it?

You can apply for a check through the Tasmanian Department of Justice here.

How long is it valid?

3 years.

Can I do child-related work in Tasmania as a visitor?

Interstate visitors can do child-related work in Tasmania without a Registration for a period of up to 28 days in any 12-month period if they hold a valid interstate check. More information.

Things to watch out for...

There are specified exceptions from the WWVP Registration, if you are in this list, you may not need to apply for the Registration. More information.

Australia Capital Territory

What's it called?

Working with Vulnerable People (WWVP) Registration.

What does it screen for?

A person's criminal history and non-conviction information. More information.

Who needs one?

An individual who works or volunteers with children or vulnerable people. More information.

Where to get it?

You can apply here.

How long is it valid?

3 years.

Can I do child-related work in ACT as a visitor?

Interstate visitors can do child-related work in ACT for up to 28 days in a calendar year if they hold a valid interstate check. More information.

Penalties

Individuals without a valid check or individuals who engage others without a valid check can faces fines of up to $28,000. More information.

Things to watch out for...

There is no registration cost to volunteers unless they are also employed in another service that is considered to be a regulated activity. A registration fee is charged if you intend to do paid work with vulnerable people or children.

The Future of Working with Children Checks

On 17 August 2015, the Royal Commission into Child Sexual Abuse released its report on Working with Children Checks.

The report noted that "each state and territory has its own scheme, and each of the eight schemes operates independently of the others. They are inconsistent and complex, and there is unnecessary duplication across the schemes. There is no integration of the schemes, and there is inadequate information sharing and monitoring of WWCC cardholders."

The Report recommends a national approach to WWCCs. "We have determined that implementing a national approach to WWCCs is overdue. For too long, governments have favoured maintaining their own systems over working together to achieve a more nationally consistent approach. We have therefore recommended a national model for WWCCs, by introducing consistent standards and establishing a centralised WWCC database to facilitate cross-border information sharing."

You can access the full report here.

However, in more than 2 years since the report was released, little progress has been made towards a national approach. In the meantime, remember to stay up-to-date and compliant with all legislation for your state. And if you're planning an interstate trip, and will be working with children in that state, make sure you're aware of the specific obligations of that state.
